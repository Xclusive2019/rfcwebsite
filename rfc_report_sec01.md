## 1. Current State Assessment

A rigorous audit of RFC's two digital properties — the main consulting site at rfcsa.co.za and the e-learning platform at rfcacademy.co.za — reveals a split identity. The consulting site carries the visual hallmarks of an earlier era in web design, while the academy presents a contemporary interface that nonetheless masks significant structural weaknesses. Both sites share a common deficiency: they are built to inform rather than to convert. In a market where ASC Food Safety publishes 2,000–5,000 word guides targeting virtually every high-value keyword in the sector [^3^], and Entecom maintains a Guidance Hub with 46+ articles, 61+ eBooks, and an active podcast series [^76^], RFC's near-total absence of indexable content represents a strategic vulnerability that extends far beyond aesthetics. The following assessment examines each site across six functional dimensions, then consolidates findings into a prioritised remediation matrix.

### 1.1 rfcsa.co.za Main Consulting Site Audit

#### 1.1.1 Visual Design Analysis

The rfcsa.co.za homepage presents a dark green, full-width hero overlay that immediately establishes a dated visual impression. The background imagery — aerial agricultural photography with a heavy green tint — lacks relevance to the urban food businesses, restaurants, and manufacturers that constitute RFC's core market in Pretoria and Gauteng. More critically, the hero section leads with "RFC Comply Cloud," RFC's proprietary compliance software, rather than a benefit-driven value proposition addressing the visitor's immediate need [^2^]. The sub-hero section promotes the e-learning academy with a generic "Take your career in the Food Industry to the next level" message that fails to specify outcomes, differentiate from competitors, or include a compelling reason to act now.

The typography relies on all-caps treatments for section headers, a convention that contemporary usability research associates with reduced readability and a perception of shouting rather than guiding. Colour contrast between the green accent elements and the dark background, while on-brand, produces a heaviness that contradicts the clarity and transparency RFC claims as core values. Navigation items on hover display green underlines that feel more reminiscent of mid-2010s template designs than current best practice. In contrast, ASC Food Safety employs a split-screen gateway allowing visitors to self-select between consulting and training paths, with professional food-industry photography and clear service taxonomy [^3^].

#### 1.1.2 Navigation Structure Review

The main navigation comprises six top-level items: Home, About, Services, Food Safety Training, Comply Cloud, and Contact [^2^]. The presence of dropdown submenus beneath Home, About, Services, Food Safety Training, and Comply Cloud indicates a hierarchical content structure, yet the absence of visual indicators (such as chevron icons) on several parent items obscures this depth from first-time visitors. The "Services" dropdown is particularly important because it represents the primary revenue path, but its discoverability depends on the user intuiting that the label is expandable.

The navigation logic reveals an organisational bias toward RFC's internal service taxonomy rather than the problem-statement language that prospects use when arriving on the site. A food business owner searching for "R638 compliance help Pretoria" or "HACCP certification cost" will not find those terms in the navigation labels. This structural mismatch directly undermines organic search relevance. Shilux, by comparison, organises its navigation around solution paths — "SME Supplier Compliance Support Programme," "QMSure Software" — that mirror buyer intent [^105^].

#### 1.1.3 Mobile Responsiveness Assessment

The rfcsa.co.za site lacks responsive optimisation for mobile devices, a critical deficiency in the South African market where mobile accounts for the majority of business-related web traffic outside of large corporate offices [^2^]. Text sizing falls below the 16px minimum recommended for body copy on touch screens, touch targets for navigation items and call-to-action buttons appear smaller than the 48×48px accessibility standard, and the full-width agricultural hero image produces slow loading times on 3G and 4G connections common in peri-urban Gauteng. The horizontal navigation collapses into a hamburger menu on smaller viewports, but testing reveals that submenu items within dropdowns become difficult to access via touch, potentially trapping mobile users who need to explore service categories. The "Pest Control Solutions" external link, visible in the header area, further complicates the mobile navigation experience by introducing an off-site destination without clear visual distinction [^2^].

#### 1.1.4 Page Speed and Technical Performance

Image assets on the homepage appear uncompressed and served in legacy formats rather than WebP, which typically reduces file sizes by 30–50% without perceptible quality loss. No content delivery network (CDN) implementation is evident, meaning visitors in Cape Town or Durban are served assets from the origin server location rather than geographically distributed edge nodes. Browser caching headers appear under-configured, forcing repeat visitors to re-download static resources on each session. The combination of uncompressed images, render-blocking CSS and JavaScript, and suboptimal server response times likely pushes First Contentful Paint beyond the three-second threshold that Google uses as a Core Web Vitals benchmark. For a B2B consulting site where decision-makers evaluate multiple providers before requesting a quote, slow loading creates an immediate credibility deficit against competitors like Entecom, whose Guidance Hub loads rapidly enough to support seamless eBook downloads [^76^].

#### 1.1.5 Lead Generation Audit

The lead generation infrastructure on rfcsa.co.za is functionally nonexistent. No blog or resource hub is present to attract organic traffic. No lead magnets — such as compliance checklists, guide downloads, or assessment tools — are offered in exchange for contact information. The site lacks newsletter signup forms, exit-intent overlays, scroll-triggered CTAs, or chat widgets. The only conversion paths visible are the "Learn More" button on the hero section, the "Read More" link under the About section, and the link to rfcacademy.co.za [^2^].

This represents a stark contrast to competitor practice. Entecom offers 61+ downloadable eBooks as lead magnets, including "8 Practical Steps to Implementing a Change Management Process in Your FSMS," captured behind email gates that build a marketing database [^77^]. Food Safety Matters provides free Regulation 638 downloads and COA application process guides that directly target the queries of new food business owners [^75^]. Hygiene Heroes attracts signups through a comprehensive free guide on R638, HACCP, and hygiene best practices [^25^]. Progress Excellence places a newsletter signup form in the header of every page, ensuring visibility regardless of scroll depth [^73^]. RFC's absence of any equivalent mechanism means that the vast majority of visitors who do not immediately decide to contact or purchase leave without a trace, creating no opportunity for remarketing or nurture sequencing.

#### 1.1.6 On-Page SEO Gaps

The site's on-page SEO foundation requires comprehensive reconstruction. No structured data markup (schema) is present — specifically missing are LocalBusiness schema for the Pretoria headquarters, Service schema for consulting offerings, and FAQPage schema that could enable rich snippet appearances in search results. The page title format appears generic rather than keyword-optimised; competitors like ASC use location-aware title structures such as "[Service] | [City] | ASC" that capture local search intent [^3^]. Meta descriptions, where present, lack compelling calls-to-action and do not include target keywords for high-value terms like "food safety training Pretoria" or "R638 compliance consulting Gauteng."

No FAQ sections appear on service pages, despite the fact that question-based queries — "What is Regulation R638?", "Who needs R638 training?", "How do I apply for a Certificate of Acceptability?" — represent some of the highest-opportunity, lowest-competition keyword targets in the South African food safety market. Internal linking between the consulting site and the academy is limited to a single promotional section, missing the opportunity to create content silos that would strengthen both domains' topical authority. The absence of a blog means the site has no mechanism for regular indexing by search engines, a critical disadvantage when ASC publishes comprehensive guides monthly and Entecom updates its Guidance Hub continuously [^3^] [^76^].

### 1.2 rfcacademy.co.za E-Learning Platform Audit

#### 1.2.1 Modern Design with Limited Catalog

The rfcacademy.co.za platform presents a markedly more contemporary visual identity than the main consulting site. The interface employs a light green and white palette with rounded card elements, modern sans-serif typography, and clear visual hierarchy [^1^]. The hero section leads with "Master Food Safety With Confidence," a benefit-oriented headline supported by SAATCA accreditation imagery and a "Certification Ready" progress indicator that suggests an active learning environment. Navigation is simplified to four items — Home, Courses, Login, Register — reducing cognitive load compared to the main site's six-item structure.

However, this polished exterior conceals a fundamental supply constraint. The academy currently offers only three courses: Food Defence Plan & Food Fraud Mitigation Plan (Intermediate, 5 hours, 4 lessons), R638 (Beginner, 6 hours, 3 lessons), and Food Safety Culture (Intermediate, 6 hours, 10 lessons) [^22^]. The course catalog page displays "Showing 3 of 3 courses" without pagination or additional category tabs, making the limited inventory immediately apparent to any visitor who navigates beyond the homepage.

#### 1.2.2 Pricing Positioning

All three courses are priced uniformly at R1,600, placing RFC in the mid-market segment of South African food safety training [^22^]. This positioning becomes meaningful only when viewed against the competitive landscape. ASC offers R638 training at R879 — nearly 45% below RFC's price — in what appears to be a strategic loss-leader approach to customer acquisition [^3^]. At the premium end, Entecom's video-based eLearning for comparable content is priced at R3,393 including VAT, justified by the production value of video-based delivery and additional SETA accreditation [^76^]. Progress Excellence occupies the budget tier at R399 for basic food safety courses, targeting a different learner segment [^73^].

RFC's uniform pricing across all courses, regardless of level or complexity, suggests a pricing strategy based on internal cost recovery rather than market-based value perception. The Food Safety Culture course, based on PAS 320 and representing a more specialised topic with 10 lessons, carries the same price as the R638 beginner course with only 3 lessons — a structure that may confuse value-sensitive buyers accustomed to tiered pricing correlating with content depth.

#### 1.2.3 Accreditation vs. Catalog Breadth

SAATCA accreditation is prominently displayed on the academy homepage, and the accreditation certificate is available for viewing — a credibility signal that distinguishes RFC from non-accredited providers [^1^]. This accreditation is particularly valuable because municipal Environmental Health Practitioners across South Africa typically require SAATCA-accredited training for Persons in Charge named on Certificates of Acceptability. However, the accreditation advantage is diluted by the catalog's narrow scope. ASC lists 65+ courses across 8 categories and 5 delivery modes [^3^]. AssureCloud offers 16+ courses spanning auditing, HACCP, ISO 22000, and R638 [^50^]. Even the relatively focused Progress Excellence catalog contains 10+ courses across food safety, quality, and HSE disciplines [^73^]. RFC's three-course offering represents a catalog gap of approximately 95% against the market leader, severely limiting cross-sell opportunities and repeat enrollment potential.

#### 1.2.4 Login/Register Functionality Without Lead Capture

The academy implements user registration and login functionality, supporting course enrollment and progress tracking [^1^]. The "Get Started Free" button on the hero suggests a freemium element, yet investigation reveals no free courses, trial lessons, or preview content available without payment. The registration flow captures name, email, and password but does not gather strategic data points — business type, location, current certification status, training budget — that would enable segmented marketing follow-up.

More critically, the academy functions as a walled garden with no content accessible to anonymous visitors. Unlike competitors who publish free resources as acquisition funnels, RFC requires account creation even to view detailed course curricula. Food Safety Matters, by contrast, offers its full blog content, free R638 PDF download, and COA application guide without registration barriers, using the free resources as trust-building steps toward paid course enrollment [^75^]. The academy's approach of gating all value behind either payment or account creation creates a high-friction experience for prospects in the awareness and consideration stages of the buyer journey.

#### 1.2.5 Missing Social Proof on Course Cards

Each course card displays difficulty level, duration, and lesson count, but all three courses show "No reviews yet" beneath their descriptions [^22^]. The testimonial section on the homepage presents three anonymous quotes attributed to "Sarah — Restaurant Manager," "Michael — Food Safety Officer," and "Lisa — Quality Assurance Manager" without surnames, company affiliations, photographs, or verifiable credentials [^1^]. The footer claims RFC Academy is the choice of "thousands of professionals," yet no enrollment counts, completion statistics, or verified learner reviews support this assertion. In a training market where Entecom cites 9,000+ graduates and 250+ company partnerships on its platform [^83^], and where prospective students increasingly rely on peer validation before committing R1,600, the absence of authentic social proof represents a significant conversion barrier.
